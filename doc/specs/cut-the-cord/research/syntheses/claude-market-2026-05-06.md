# Market & competitive synthesis (post-DR, 2026-05-06)

## TL;DR (5 bullets)

- **Whitespace confirmed.** No incumbent ships the full local-first loop "messy voice in → cited checklist + procedure excerpt + structured incident log + zero-egress proof" for dangerous-jobs operators. Adjacent offline tools (ArcGIS Field Maps, KoboCollect, TAK, Sahana Eden) prove the field-offline category is normal but stop short of voice incident intake + grounded LLM answers. (`inbox/01-airgap-incident-copilot.md` §"What exists today and where the gap remains")
- **Safe medical framing exists and is well-precedented.** Pocket RAG (arXiv 2602.13229) hits 94.5% physical-first-aid accuracy and 97.0% psychological-first-aid accuracy in <2 GB RAM with TTFT cut from 14.2s → 3.7s; this gives PoliSa a citable, defensible claim surface — *cited checklist assistant for trained responders*, not "AI doctor". (`inbox/02-offline-first-aid-healthcare.md` §"Research landscape")
- **Consumer wearable category is a graveyard.** Humane Pin (HP acqui-shutdown), Rabbit R1 (cloud-bound, mocked), Friend / Bee / Plaud / Limitless all bleed audio to third-party AI providers despite "privacy" branding — Limitless's own privacy policy admits it shares recordings with third-party AI vendors. PoliSa's B2B safety-wearable does not share these failure modes (clear ROI, mandated workflows, employer pays, compliance moat). (`inbox/03-wearables-embedded-local-ai.md` §"Market landscape" + `inbox/07-competitive-go-to-market.md` §"Wearable cautionary examples")
- **Best beachhead is utility storm-response line crews, not firefighters.** DR-07's ranking puts utilities #1 (DOE $2.5B grid resilience program, NERC 2026 cloud-risk roadmap, OT zero-trust mandates, aging workforce) ahead of fire/EMS, which has the best demo theater but worst procurement velocity. Firefighters are the lighthouse pitch; utilities are the wedge. (`inbox/07-competitive-go-to-market.md` §"Ranked best wedge markets")
- **The 2026 stack is ready.** Gemma 4 (edge), EmbeddingGemma (on-device RAG), Picovoice Orca (offline streaming TTS), MLX-Whisper, sqlite-vec, Foundry Local GA, OpenVINO GenAI on NPU — every required component now has 2026 first-party support. The pitch no longer needs to argue feasibility; it needs to argue *which buyer cares enough to pay*. (`inbox/07-competitive-go-to-market.md` §"Market and adoption signals")

## White-space cells (the 3 strongest)

### 1. Utility storm-response & substation switching crews (lineworkers, troublemen)

- **Vertical.** Electric utility field operations: storm restoration, switching, substation maintenance, vegetation/wildfire inspection. Buyer = head of T&D field ops, VP field services, utility safety/training leader, or OT/security leader. User = lineworker, troubleman, substation tech, vegetation inspector, contractor crew on storm response.
- **Why no incumbent.** Augmentir / Beekeeper / Sidekick are cloud-first horizontal connected-worker suites — they don't offer a deterministic offline mode for the moment of risk. RealWear and Vuzix ship voice-first hardware but the AI features (translation, remote expert) assume connectivity, and the procurement is hardware-CAPEX-first. ArcGIS Field Maps gives the map but not the *next step*. Protex AI watches the floor with cameras but doesn't ride along with the worker. HPE and Elastic ship the air-gapped infra but not the worker-facing application. Nobody ships a software-only, fully-offline, cited-procedure voice copilot for the moment a tornado just ripped through substation B and there's no LTE.
- **What PoliSa would build.** Voice intake → local retrieval over the utility's approved switching procedures + lockout/tagout (29 CFR 1910.147) + arc-flash (NFPA 70E) + employer-specific substation SOPs → cited checklist + structured incident-log JSON shaped to the customer's system of record (Maximo, ServiceNow, custom). Optional "tailboard mode" — pre-job-briefing checklist generated from the work order + 1910.269 + NFPA 70E. Sync deferred until the truck reconnects to LTE/Wi-Fi; sync includes a hash of the procedure version actually used so audit can confirm the worker had the current revision.
- **60-second demo angle judges will remember.** Stage scenario, fully airplane mode: storm just hit substation B. Lineworker speaks: *"flash and one guy is down near panel B, smoke, power status unknown."* In under 10 seconds: cited NFPA 70E excerpt about verifying de-energization before approach, scene-safety priority list, escalation banner ("call dispatch now — location, victim count, electrical hazard"), and a structured incident-log JSON ready for sync. Then the demo presenter opens the JSON file in front of the judges and shows that every checklist item resolves to a specific OSHA/NFPA section number with a reproducible chunk_id. *Then* they show `scripts/netproof.sh` reporting zero packets out.
- **Regulatory or connectivity reason cloud loses.**
    - **Connectivity.** FCC disaster reporting + 2025 CRS analysis confirm cellular outage during disasters is the *norm*, not the exception. The same storm that creates the urgent field work removes the connectivity needed for cloud copilots.
    - **Compliance.** NERC's 2026 roadmap explicitly raises cloud-service risk in the power sector; CISA's April 2026 OT zero-trust guidance pushes utilities toward segmentation and away from casual cloud routing of operational data.
    - **Buyer budget.** DOE has $2.5B over 5 years specifically tagged as grid resilience, plus an additional ~$1.9B announced for power-infrastructure investment in 2026. The buyer has budget, has procurement scaffolding, and has aging-workforce / training-acceleration / continuity pain. (`inbox/07-competitive-go-to-market.md` §"Buyer segments")

### 2. Hazmat / chemical-splash first-response in oil & gas, chemical plants, pharma (ATEX Zone 1)

- **Vertical.** Oil & gas, refining, chemical plants, pharma, food processing. Buyer = HSE director, plant manager, EHS compliance lead. User = process operator, maintenance tech, first-response team member, lone worker on swing shift.
- **Why no incumbent.** RealWear has the ATEX-certified hardware but its AI features assume connectivity to the partner-software cloud and the remote-expert call infrastructure; Vuzix Shield same. HPE / Elastic ship the air-gapped infra but not the worker app. Onsight / Iristick are remote-collaboration products built around the cloud video call. Field1st and Fulcrum capture inspection forms after the fact. Nobody ships a packaged voice → NIOSH Pocket Guide → cited chemical first-aid + escalation pipeline that runs on a rugged tablet or the worker's laptop in airplane mode and emits an OSHA-shaped incident log.
- **What PoliSa would build.** Pre-loaded NIOSH Pocket Guide chunked by chemical / hazard / IDLH / first-aid; OSHA HazCom (29 CFR 1910.1200) + Emergency Action Plan (1910.38) + Process Safety Management (1910.119) excerpts; plant-specific SOPs and SDSes loaded at deployment. Voice intake captures chemical name (or visible label OCR if camera available), exposure route, symptoms, victim count, location → retrieves cited first-aid steps + IDLH guidance + "do not do" list → escalation banner with the specific call tree the plant has approved → structured incident-log JSON aligned to OSHA 300 / 301 reporting. The pitch leans hard on the safety claim surface: this is *not* a triage tool, it is a *cited reference retrieval tool* that reads the same way the plant safety binder reads, except the worker can talk to it with one hand.
- **60-second demo angle judges will remember.** Stage scenario, airplane mode: *"solvent splash on forearm, eye irritation, unlabeled container, one person, pump room B."* In under 10 seconds: NIOSH chemical first-aid quote (rinse with running water 15 minutes, remove contaminated clothing), the explicit *"do not attempt to identify the chemical from memory — treat as worst case"*, *"call site emergency lead now"*, and a JSON log with location + symptoms + suspected hazard + immediate actions + escalation status. The dramatic beat is that even *without* knowing what the chemical was, the assistant gave correct, cited first-response procedure — because that's what NIOSH guidance says to do.
- **Regulatory or connectivity reason cloud loses.**
    - **Physics.** ATEX Zone 1 environments restrict wireless emissions; intrinsically-safe devices have hardened comms but stricter power budgets, and corp-IT often blocks LTE pass-through into the process area.
    - **Time.** Chemical incidents are time-critical: NIOSH and OSHA both prescribe action timelines measured in seconds for eye/skin contact. Cloud round-trip latency is unsafe.
    - **Licensing.** NIOSH Pocket Guide is *public domain* (CDC / ATSDR public-domain notice), as is OSHA's federal publication. Zero licensing risk on the corpus. (`inbox/01-airgap-incident-copilot.md` §"Demo-safe preload pack" + `inbox/02-offline-first-aid-healthcare.md` §"Public data sources")

### 3. Underground mining maintenance & post-incident response

- **Vertical.** Underground coal, hardrock, salt, and metal mining; also surface-mining post-incident response. Buyer = mine safety officer, MSHA compliance lead, operations VP. User = miner, maintenance crew lead, mine-rescue team member.
- **Why no incumbent.** The connectivity story is structurally the strongest of any vertical: NIOSH explicitly notes underground radio-coverage verification is "difficult and time-consuming"; roof falls, explosions, and water ingress take infrastructure down. RealWear is voice-first and ATEX/MSHA-rated but its AI loop still depends on uplink. Qwake is fire-service-focused. Nobody ships an offline cited-procedure assistant scoped to MSHA Part 75 (underground coal) / Part 48 (training) / Part 56-57 (surface and underground metal/non-metal) plus the mine's own MSHA-mandated Emergency Response Plan (ERP) under the MINER Act.
- **What PoliSa would build.** MSHA-approved emergency response procedures + NIOSH mine-safety guidance + site-specific MSHA-required ERPs preloaded. Voice intake captures the incident category (fire / methane / roof fall / inundation / electrical / casualty) and victim status → retrieves cited procedure (e.g., 30 CFR 75.1502 mine emergency evacuation) + the mine's specific ERP excerpt → escalation banner with MSHA notification clock ("MSHA Section 103(j) requires immediate notification — clock started"). Particular emphasis on "what to do during the first 10 minutes after an incident" because that is where regulated procedures save lives and connectivity is most likely gone.
- **60-second demo angle judges will remember.** Stage scenario, airplane mode: *"smell of smoke from conveyor 3, two miners not accounted for, primary radio dark."* In under 10 seconds: cited MSHA evacuation procedure, the mine's specific ERP self-rescuer instruction, the do/don't list (don't re-enter, don't attempt rescue without team + apparatus, do call MSHA within 15 minutes), the JSON log with conveyor-3 location + missing miner count + ignition source classification + MSHA notification timestamp. The judges remember it because they hear the words "two miners not accounted for" and immediately understand why the cloud is not the answer.
- **Regulatory or connectivity reason cloud loses.**
    - **Regulation.** 30 CFR Part 75 Subpart J + the MINER Act of 2006 emergency-comm regulations exist *because* underground comms fail; the same regulations require MSHA notification within 15 minutes of incidents that pose an imminent danger.
    - **Worker safety baseline.** ILO 2025 safety report explicitly highlights remote supervision and digital tools in mining as a way to reduce worker exposure to hazards.
    - **Risk.** BLS 2024 fatal injury rate for mining / quarrying / oil-&-gas extraction = 13.8 per 100K FTE workers, materially above utilities and manufacturing. The lives-per-dollar math is hard for a buyer to argue against. (`inbox/07-competitive-go-to-market.md` §"Mining and hazardous industrial")

## Consumer-wearable failure modes we sidestep

1. **Humane AI Pin failure: the cloud servers shut off and the device bricked overnight.** Humane sold the IP to HP in early 2024; the AI Pin's intelligence lived in someone else's data center, so when the service ended, the hardware became a paperweight. *Why our B2B safety-wearable doesn't share this failure:* the workflow is on-device by contract and the employer (utility, mine, refinery) is the buyer — operational-continuity guarantees are explicit in procurement (and in some cases NERC-CIP/CISA-mandated). If our cloud disappears, the device keeps working on its main flow. *Clear ROI:* the buyer has already counted the cost of an OSHA recordable; the device pays for itself before the cloud is even a question. (`inbox/03-wearables-embedded-local-ai.md` §"Market landscape" + §"Privacy narrative")
2. **Rabbit R1 failure: hard-to-justify ROI; was a consumer toy looking for a job.** The R1 was reviewed widely as "what does this do that my phone doesn't?" — the consumer-product gravitational pull always points back to "buy a better phone". *Why B2B doesn't share this:* the ROI is mandated and quantifiable. OSHA recordable injuries cost the employer ~$42K direct + ~$48K indirect each (US BLS / Liberty Mutual safety index inputs); MSHA findings carry per-violation civil penalties and public-record reputational cost; NERC-CIP violations carry per-day fines up to $1M; downtime hours in upstream oil & gas can be measured in 6-figure numbers per hour. *Mandated workflows:* the buyer doesn't ask "is this useful?", they ask "does this satisfy our SMS / SHE / OT-zero-trust posture?". (`inbox/07-competitive-go-to-market.md` §"Anti-patterns")
3. **Friend pendant failure: parasocial / "always listening" creepy-vibes; bystander privacy.** The launch trailer leaned into companionship; the public reaction in social-media testing was largely negative because *ambient capture in social settings is creepy*. *Why B2B doesn't share this:* the work environment is *already* recorded (CCTV on industrial floor, bodycams on emergency responders, dashcams in utility trucks), the workflow is *already* mandated by the employer's safety management system, capture is push-to-talk + visible capture state, and the worker is on the clock — there is no "bystander" who hasn't already consented to industrial-site recording norms. *Captive distribution:* the device is issued by the employer, not chosen by the worker, so social-acceptability is not the gating factor; OSHA / regulatory acceptance is. (`inbox/03-wearables-embedded-local-ai.md` §"Privacy narrative")
4. **Limitless / Bee failure: privacy-branded wearables whose own privacy policies admit cloud + third-party AI sharing.** Limitless's privacy policy explicitly says they share recordings or transcripts with third-party AI providers for transcription and summarization. Bee's privacy notice covers service-side processing without fully publishing the inference split. *Why B2B doesn't share this:* enterprise procurement is a contractual moat. The customer's own legal team writes the no-egress clause; the compliance officer writes the data-residency clause; the OT-security architect writes the segmentation clause. There is no "policy footnote" because the customer wrote the policy. *Compliance moat:* a competitor who wants to bid against us on the same RFP must be able to clear the same procurement gate — and a cloud-based competitor literally cannot pass the OT-zero-trust scoring rubric. (`inbox/07-competitive-go-to-market.md` §"Wearable cautionary examples")
5. **Plaud / generic note-taker failure: cloud transcription for "AI productivity" — generic horizontal value prop, can't beat ChatGPT-on-phone.** Plaud's docs say recordings transfer to the app and are processed in secure cloud storage — and even if that worked perfectly, the value prop is "I have a podcast of my meetings now". The user's existing phone with a free ChatGPT account does almost the same thing. *Why B2B for dangerous jobs doesn't share this:* compliance moat (OSHA / MSHA / NFPA / NERC procedures, ICS / NIMS forms shaped to specific report numbers), domain corpus (NIOSH Pocket Guide, OSHA EAP, NFPA 70E, customer SOPs the cloud LLMs literally do not have in training), and *employer-pays-for-the-device* economics so the alternative isn't "use my phone for free", it's "use the device the employer issued". The product is *not* a thinner ChatGPT — it's a cited workflow over an approved corpus that the cloud LLMs don't have and can't get. (`inbox/01-airgap-incident-copilot.md` §"Demo-safe preload pack" + `inbox/03-wearables-embedded-local-ai.md` §"Market landscape")

## Safe medical/first-aid framing

### Three "do not say" claims (exact phrases to avoid in the pitch)
1. **"This diagnoses emergencies"** / "differential diagnosis" / "disease probability" — directly inside the FDA 2026 CDS guidance device-trigger language. (`inbox/02-offline-first-aid-healthcare.md` §"Compliance and claims to avoid")
2. **"This triages patients"** / "assigns ESI / CTAS / acuity" / "prioritizes dispatch" — the EU AI Act recital on emergency services explicitly classifies emergency-call evaluation, dispatch prioritization, and emergency healthcare patient triage as *high-risk*. Saying these words out loud puts the product inside that regime on stage. (`inbox/02-offline-first-aid-healthcare.md` §"Compliance and claims to avoid")
3. **"Replaces clinicians / dispatchers / local protocols"** or "Clinically validated" or "Proven to improve outcomes" — WHO governance principles emphasize human oversight and accountability; emergency-care benchmark literature still shows undertriage and unsafe outputs in much larger frontier models. Claiming validation we haven't earned is the fastest credibility loss with judges. (`inbox/02-offline-first-aid-healthcare.md` §"Compliance and claims to avoid")

### Three "always say" claims (exact phrases to lean on)
1. **"Cited procedural assistant for trained responders working in degraded-connectivity environments"** — this matches the FDA non-device CDS criteria (recommendations not directives; basis surfaced; doesn't analyze signals/images; works with information clinicians normally communicate). (`inbox/02-offline-first-aid-healthcare.md` §"safe hackathon framing")
2. **"Helps trained people find the right cited checklist faster when the network is down"** — verb is "helps", subject is "trained people", object is "checklist", modifier is "cited". Every word matters. (`inbox/02-offline-first-aid-healthcare.md` §"safe hackathon framing")
3. **"Always escalates to humans; never takes control of equipment; leaves an auditable trail of what it suggested and why"** — directly maps to NIST AI RMF + EU AI Act human-oversight + deployer-obligation framing. (`inbox/02-offline-first-aid-healthcare.md` §"safe hackathon framing" + `inbox/01-airgap-incident-copilot.md` §"Safety and liability framing")

## Appendix C — Anti-pattern decoder (what each consumer-wearable failure actually teaches us)

> One paragraph each, so the pitch-pair can quote the lesson without sounding glib.

**Humane AI Pin (HP shutdown).** The Pin proved that a wearable whose intelligence lives in someone else's data center is not a wearable; it's a thin client with a battery. When Humane sold the IP to HP in early 2024 and the cloud went away, the device stopped being useful. The lesson is not "wearables are bad"; the lesson is *cloud-bound intelligence is service-fragile, and service fragility kills hardware*. Our product is on-device by contract, so a vendor change, an API price hike, or a corporate acquisition cannot brick it. (`inbox/03-wearables-embedded-local-ai.md` §"Market landscape" + §"Privacy narrative")

**Rabbit R1.** The R1 promised a "Large Action Model" that would learn workflows from demonstration; what shipped was a cloud-routed wrapper around third-party APIs. The lesson is that a consumer-toy ROI ("an AI agent that does cool stuff") loses to a B2B ROI ("an OSHA recordable injury costs the employer $42K direct + $48K indirect") every time. Our buyer doesn't need to be entertained; they need a quantifiable line item. (DR-03 implied + `inbox/07-competitive-go-to-market.md` §"Buyer segments")

**Friend pendant (Avi Schiffmann).** The pendant's marketing leans into parasocial / companionship language; the public reaction to the launch trailer was largely negative because *ambient capture in social settings is creepy*. The lesson for safety wearables is that capture must be *episodic, push-to-talk, visibly indicated, and obviously work-context only*. Our product never listens ambiently; the worker explicitly initiates capture for an explicit incident. (`inbox/03-wearables-embedded-local-ai.md` §"Privacy narrative")

**Bee AI / Plaud / Limitless.** All three frame as "private memory" but their privacy policies disclose cloud processing and third-party AI sharing. Limitless explicitly: "we share recordings or transcripts with third-party AI providers for transcription and summarization". The lesson is that *self-described privacy* dies under one slide of policy text, but *contractually-mandated privacy* (the customer's own legal team writes the no-egress clause) does not. Enterprise procurement is the moat, not consumer trust. (`inbox/07-competitive-go-to-market.md` §"Wearable cautionary examples")

**Even Realities G2 (translation glasses).** Even's own support page admits all functions need internet. The lesson is cosmetic-grade tech can ship without the offline mode and still find consumer buyers, but *industrial buyers will not accept that compromise* — RealWear ships ATEX precisely because the alternative is unsafe. Industrial buyers care about offline by reflex. (`inbox/03-wearables-embedded-local-ai.md` §"Hardware constraints")
