# Offline On-Device AI for First Aid and Emergency Guidance

## Executive summary

The strongest hackathon direction is **not** an “AI doctor,” autonomous triage agent, or diagnostic app. The most defensible direction is an **offline, cited checklist assistant** that retrieves and quotes from a fixed on-device corpus of public guidelines, shows the exact source passage, asks a small number of structured questions, and escalates early to local emergency services or clinician review. That framing fits what the current evidence actually supports: on-device RAG and small models are now fast enough for a convincing demo, but the medical safety literature still shows omission errors, unsafe answers, undertriage, prompt sensitivity, and poor self-awareness even in much larger frontier models. citeturn7view0turn19view3turn22view0turn27view0turn35view0

My synthesis is that judges are most likely to accept a demo framed as **“offline access to cited emergency guidance when the network is down”** because that is useful in remote clinics, rural settings, and disasters while avoiding the claims that push a system toward regulated medical-device territory. The regulatory and policy signals are clear: software that gives specific risk scores, probabilities, time-critical directives, or emergency triage decisions is much harder to defend than software that presents transparent reference-backed options for a human to review. citeturn36view0turn36view1turn37view0turn37view1

## Research landscape

The most on-point paper I found is **Pocket RAG: On-Device RAG for First Aid Guidance in Offline Mobile Environments** from early 2026. It is explicitly built for disconnected first-aid use on Android, works within an approximately **2 GB** memory budget, stores an **INT8 vector index of about 120 MB for 8,000 chunks**, reduces retrieval candidates from thousands to **fewer than 50** through lexical prefiltering, and uses selective context compression plus KV-cache quantization to reduce **time-to-first-token from 14.2 seconds to 3.7 seconds**. On a 200-question WHO-derived physical first-aid set, its best configuration reached **94.5%** accuracy; on a 200-question psychological first-aid set, the best result reached **97.0%**. The paper is a research prototype rather than a clinical deployment, but it is exactly the kind of evidence that makes an offline checklist assistant plausible on current phones. citeturn6view0turn7view0

More broadly, the 2024–2026 edge RAG literature shows that the systems problem is becoming tractable. **EdgeRAG** targets memory-constrained edge devices with online indexing and adaptive caching so evaluated datasets fit memory with lower latency than a baseline IVF index. **MobileRAG** then pushes further, reporting a fully on-device pipeline with lower memory, latency, and power consumption than conventional mobile RAG baselines; its authors summarize gains of **10.7–54.5% lower memory**, **1.18–1.41× faster TTFT**, and **24.4–40.2% lower power consumption** in end-to-end RAG, while retrieval-only power consumption falls **47–67%** versus Naive-RAG and **61–75%** versus EdgeRAG across QA datasets. In a hardware-oriented medical-edge paper for wearable agents, a hierarchical two-stage retrieval design cuts memory access by nearly **50%**, computation by **75%**, and reports **177.76 μJ/query** for 1 MB retrieval in simulation, showing where the next efficiency gains are likely to come from even though that work is not a phone-ready app. citeturn16view0turn14view0turn15view2turn15view4turn17view0turn13search2

Outside first aid specifically, the best evidence for local medical SLM practicality comes from **HealthSLM-Bench** and related medical SLM work. HealthSLM-Bench deploys tuned SLMs on an iPhone 15 Pro Max and reports major latency differences: **TinyLlama-1.1B** at **1.37 s TTFT**, **1.79 s total time**, and **5.17 GB RAM**; **Phi-3-mini-4k** at **6.39 s TTFT**, **7.61 s total time**, and **6.48 GB RAM**; versus **Llama-2-7b** at **29.12 s TTFT**, **57.43 s total time**, and **7.15 GB RAM**. The same benchmark also surfaces important caveats: class imbalance remains hard, few-shot prompting can collapse, and efficiency gains do not automatically imply robustness. Meanwhile, **Meerkat**, a 2025 family of medical SLMs trained on textbook-derived reasoning traces, shows that compact models can improve substantially on medical reasoning while remaining local-deployment friendly; the authors report that the 7B/8B models can run on a single **24 GB RTX 3090** and outperform prior open-weight models across six benchmarks. citeturn19view3turn18view0turn20view0

A separate 2025 benchmark focused on emergency departments is also relevant for hackathon design: general-domain SLMs often outperformed medically fine-tuned peers on ED-oriented tasks. For example, **Phi3-small-8k** achieved **0.776** accuracy on PubMedQA versus **0.752** for Phi-3-medical-instruct and **0.740** for Medical-Llama-3-8B; on MedMCQA and MedQA-4Options, general models were also usually at or near the top. That result argues for a practical design principle: **keep the model small and competent, and move domain specificity into retrieval and citations rather than betting the whole demo on a medically fine-tuned generator**. citeturn25view0turn26view0turn26view1turn26view4

## Benchmarks and reported metrics

The best high-confidence metrics for an emergency-guidance demo are therefore these. For **accuracy**, Pocket RAG reports up to **94.5%** on WHO-derived physical first-aid multiple-choice questions and **97.0%** on WHO-derived psychological first-aid questions under RAG-plus-reranking, with weaker base models improving dramatically but still remaining less trustworthy than stronger small models. For **latency**, Pocket RAG reports **3.7 s TTFT** on a Samsung Galaxy S23 Ultra after optimizations, while HealthSLM-Bench shows tuned SLMs can range from approximately **1–6 seconds TTFT** on a modern phone depending on model size. For **memory**, Pocket RAG is designed around an approximate **2 GB** Android application ceiling, while HealthSLM-Bench measured **5.17–6.48 GB** RAM usage for larger phone-side models on iPhone. For **energy**, MobileRAG is the clearest source: on Qwen-2.5 0.5B it reports **24.71 J/query** on SQuAD, **55.21 J/query** on HotpotQA, and **51.30 J/query** on TriviaQA for the full RAG pipeline, all below Naive-RAG and EdgeRAG baselines. citeturn7view0turn19view3turn14view0

The equally important story is **failure modes**. In Pocket RAG, RAG reduced hallucination relative to vanilla generation, but the paper is explicit that retrieval quality cannot fully compensate for a weak base model. In the 2026 emergency-care benchmark, nearly all large models **undertriaged**, and qualitative review found omission of secondary diagnoses, premature anchoring, vague plans, and unsafe recommendations. In public-chatbot evaluation on **222 patient-posed questions**, problematic responses ranged from **21.6% to 43.2%** and clearly **unsafe responses** from **5% to 13%**. In BLS scenarios, GPT-4 did relatively well for adults (**85%** correctness) but all tested chatbots scored **below 44%** in infant scenarios and none adhered consistently to BLS guidelines. In a heart-attack first-aid study, full congruence with guideline checklists ranged only from **7.3% to 16.8%**, with many superfluous or inconsistent directives. citeturn7view0turn27view0turn22view0turn23view0turn23view1

There are also deeper systemic risks that matter for your pitch. Medical LLM research in 2024–2026 shows susceptibility to **targeted misinformation attacks**, **data-poisoning**, and a lack of robust **metacognition** about when the model is uncertain. One study showed that changing just **1.1% of model weights** could inject false biomedical facts into output; another showed that replacing just **0.001% of training tokens** with misinformation could produce more harmful models; and a metacognition study concluded that current models show a significant gap between perceived and actual medical reasoning ability. Those findings strongly support a design that does **not** rely on open-web generation in real time and instead stays inside a reviewed, versioned local corpus. citeturn21search0turn21search2turn21search1

## Public data sources for a safe demo

The best retrieval corpus is public guidance from entity["organization","World Health Organization","un health agency"] and entity["organization","International Federation of Red Cross and Red Crescent Societies","humanitarian network"]. The WHO/ICRC **Basic Emergency Care** manual is explicitly described as an **open-access** course for first-contact providers in limited-resource settings, and the PDF search result identifies a **CC BY-NC-SA 3.0 IGO** license. The WHO **Emergency Care Toolkit** is also described as **open access** and includes emergency checklists, triage, resuscitation-area guidance, pocket guides, and referral forms. The WHO **Prehospital Toolkit** adds community first-aid response content aligned with BEC and aimed at community responders and basic ambulance providers. The IFRC **International First Aid, Resuscitation and Education Guidelines 2025** are publicly downloadable and intended to harmonize first-aid practice across the movement. Together, these are strong sources for a judge-friendly, cited, offline knowledge base. citeturn8view1turn5search0turn8view2turn8view3turn8view0

For communication support, the **Emergency Multilingual Phrasebook** from the entity["organization","British Red Cross","uk humanitarian charity"] covers common medical questions and terms in **36 languages** to support first contact while an interpreter is reached. For disaster settings, the **Mobile REMM** resource from the entity["organization","U.S. Department of Health and Human Services","us health department"] is a good adjacent precedent: it is evidence based, intended for radiological and nuclear emergencies, and downloadable in advance so it remains available if the internet is inaccessible. Those examples show that “offline emergency knowledge on a phone” is already an acceptable product shape when the content is authoritative and the scope is clear. citeturn29view0turn29view1turn38search1turn38search4

For **evaluation**, not retrieval, use public QA and safety datasets. **FirstAidQA** contains **5,500** synthetic, human-validated first-aid QA pairs and is useful for stress-testing SLM behavior, but because it is synthetic and derived from a first-aid book, it is safer as an internal evaluation or tuning seed than as the patient-facing source of truth. **MedMCQA**, **MedQA**, and **PubMedQA** are useful for benchmarking knowledge and retrieval pipelines, but they are exam or research QA sets, not front-line first-aid guidance. For safety regression, the public **HealthAdvice** dataset released with the patient-safety study, **MedSafetyBench**, and **HealthBench** are valuable because they evaluate open-ended healthcare behavior, not just multiple-choice knowledge. citeturn12search12turn12search15turn12search6turn12search3turn12search5turn22view0turn28search0turn28search9turn28search1turn28search7

The practical implication is simple: **use authoritative public guidance for retrieval, and use QA and safety benchmarks only for offline evaluation**. Do not build the demo on scraped web health content, because that is exactly the path the safety literature warns against. citeturn21search0turn21search2turn22view0

## safe hackathon framing

The safest and strongest framing is:

**“An offline, cited emergency-guidance assistant that helps a responder find the right checklist fast when the network is down.”** It should be presented as a **retrieval and checklist tool**, not as a doctor, diagnostician, or autonomous triage system. That framing is supported by the state of the art: on-device RAG is fast enough to look credible, but the patient-safety and emergency-medicine literature still shows unsafe generations, undertriage, omission errors, and prompt sensitivity. citeturn7view0turn14view0turn22view0turn27view0

A judge-friendly demo would look like this. The user picks a role such as **lay responder**, **community health worker**, or **remote clinic nurse**; chooses a scenario such as choking, burns, bleeding, shock, chest pain, seizure, psychological first aid, or radiation incident; and the app returns a **short checklist generated only from an on-device corpus**, with every step linked to the exact source paragraph and a visible “when to call emergency services / escalate now” banner. The app can also switch language or show a pretranslated phrasebook view. That is genuinely useful in rural and disaster contexts while keeping the system explainable and bounded. citeturn8view1turn8view2turn8view3turn8view0turn29view0turn38search1

The three strongest demo principles are these:

- **Citations first.** Every instruction shown in the UI should link to the exact WHO/IFRC/official source passage used to produce it. This aligns with transparent CDS-style behavior and makes hallucinations easier to spot. citeturn36view1turn35view0
- **Checklist, not diagnosis.** Ask only enough questions to route the user to a checklist or escalation path; do not infer disease probability, assign a triage score, or choose among diagnoses. citeturn36view1turn37view1
- **Escalate early.** The product should explicitly tell users when it cannot determine the next step, when symptoms suggest a time-critical emergency, and when local protocols override generic guidance. That is more consistent with WHO’s emphasis on ethics, human rights, and accountable human oversight. citeturn35view0turn34view0
- **Use canned public scenarios in the demo.** Show offline mode, airplane mode, low-connectivity mode, and multilingual retrieval with synthetic or public cases rather than live patient data. That keeps the demonstration truthful and avoids privacy theater. citeturn18view0turn25view0turn35view0

If you want a one-sentence pitch, use: **“We built an offline emergency checklist assistant that retrieves versioned, source-cited guidance from trusted public medical manuals directly on-device, so responders can still access the right steps when connectivity fails.”** That is substantially safer and more credible than “AI doctor for emergencies.” citeturn8view1turn8view2turn8view0turn7view0

## Compliance and claims to avoid

From a compliance standpoint, the red lines are clear. The entity["organization","U.S. Food and Drug Administration","us regulator"] says non-device CDS must meet all four criteria: it must not analyze medical images/signals/patterns; it must work with information normally communicated between clinicians; it must provide **recommendations or options rather than a specific directive**; and it must provide the basis for the recommendation so the clinician does not rely primarily on the software. The FDA’s device examples explicitly include **risk scores**, **probability of disease**, and **time-critical outputs**. The same guidance also notes that software intended for patients or caregivers is treated more cautiously. citeturn36view0turn36view1turn36view2

In the EU, the entity["organization","European Commission","eu executive body"] states that AI-based software intended for medical purposes is part of the **high-risk** regime, and the AI Act recital on emergency services specifically says that systems used to **evaluate and classify emergency calls**, **dispatch emergency response priority**, or perform **emergency healthcare patient triage** should be classified as high-risk because they make decisions in critical life-and-health situations. That means you should not pitch the system as autonomous triage, dispatch prioritization, or a substitute for emergency clinicians. citeturn37view0turn37view1

In practical pitch language, you should **never claim** the following:

- **“This diagnoses emergencies.”** Do not claim diagnosis, differential diagnosis ranking, or disease probability. citeturn36view1turn37view0
- **“This triages patients safely”** or **“This assigns ESI/CTAS/acuity.”** Triage and emergency prioritization are exactly the kinds of high-risk functions regulators call out, and current benchmark evidence still shows undertriage and unsafe outputs. citeturn37view1turn27view0
- **“This replaces clinicians, dispatchers, or local protocols.”** WHO guidance emphasizes ethics, accountability, and human oversight, and the emergency-care literature does not support replacement claims. citeturn35view0turn34view0turn27view0
- **“Clinically validated” or “proven to improve outcomes”** unless you actually ran a real study. Research prototypes like Pocket RAG are promising, but they are not clinical trials. citeturn7view0turn14view0turn18view0
- **“Works on any medical question.”** The public-safety literature shows omission errors, unsafe answers, misinformation vulnerability, and prompt sensitivity even in large models. citeturn22view0turn23view0turn23view1turn21search0turn21search1turn21search2

The right claim surface is much narrower and safer: **educational aid**, **checklist retrieval**, **source citation**, **offline access**, **human-in-the-loop**, **designed for reference and escalation**, and **not for diagnosis or definitive treatment decisions**. citeturn36view1turn35view0turn37view0

## Open questions and limitations

Several of the most relevant on-device papers, including Pocket RAG, MobileRAG, HealthSLM-Bench, EdgeRAG, and the ED SLM benchmark, are **recent research papers or preprints rather than deployed clinical products**, so their reported metrics are encouraging but not equivalent to field validation. citeturn6view0turn14view0turn18view0turn16view0turn25view0

The evidence base is also uneven. We now have good early data on **accuracy, TTFT, memory, and some query-level energy**, but much less on **battery drain over long sessions, multilingual first-aid safety, offline speech UX, adverse-event reporting, and performance under noisy real-world prompts from distressed non-expert users**. The patient-facing studies strongly suggest these factors matter, but the specific mobile emergency literature has not yet closed those gaps. citeturn22view0turn23view0turn23view1

Finally, for content reuse, the WHO BEC material is explicitly open access and the WHO emergency toolkit is explicitly open access, but I did not verify a permissive redistribution license for every public document page I reviewed, including the IFRC 2025 guideline page. For a hackathon demo, the safest pattern is therefore to **store excerpts with attribution for retrieval, preserve source metadata, and avoid wholesale republication of full proprietary or unclear-license content**. citeturn5search0turn8view1turn8view2turn8view0
